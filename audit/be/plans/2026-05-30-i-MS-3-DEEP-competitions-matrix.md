## How to read each entry

Each program is normalized to **9 fields** (heavy emphasis on 6, 7, 8):

1. **WHO** — Legal entity / operating org / parent ownership chain
2. **TIMELINE** — Past 5 cycles + next 5 cycles
3. **URL REF** — Official program homepage
4. **URL TO ENTER** — Submission portal
5. **TERMS OF ENTRY** — Eligibility + application requirements
6. **🔴 IP OWNERSHIP — VERBATIM CLAUSE TEXT** (the load-bearing field)
7. **SHARING REQUIREMENTS** — What must be public after winning
8. **DO WE OWN OUR IP — FINAL ANSWER** — Traffic-light verdict
9. **PROOF IN LAYMAN'S TERMS** — Past 5 winners + outcomes

Traffic-light verdict scale:
- 🟢 **CLEAN** — Joey owns everything; only minimal "you're a participant" attribution
- 🟡 **MODERATE** — Joey owns the tech; grants organizer some promotional / publicity rights
- 🟠 **CAUTION** — Submission *materials* (deck, video, demo) carry a perpetual license to organizer; underlying technology unaffected, BUT the application content itself is theirs
- 🔴 **DANGER** — IP transfer, mandatory open-source release, or organizer claims "submission becomes property of" without limit

   >

   **Reading:** This is one of the most aggressive submission-license clauses on the public internet — perpetual, irrevocable, sublicensable, royalty-free, worldwide, "for any purpose that we choose" — including the right to exploit **patent rights** in your submission. The clause attaches to **any material submitted via the Services**, which includes the Battlefield application. The "classified ad" clause is an outright assignment for ad content. You retain ownership of the **invention**. TechCrunch cannot license your patent to a third party in a binding way unless your submission *exposed the patent claim* (e.g., you put the algorithm in the deck). So **stay generic in the deck**, keep the technical details out, and the submission license is a manageable cost.

   **The bad news:** Whatever's in the deck, TechCrunch and "anyone authorized by us" can use forever, anywhere. They can resell it. They can sublicense it. They can train AI on it. They can use your demo video in their next subscription product.

## 5. NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography — 🔴 (the most extreme sharing requirement on Earth)

1. **WHO.** **NIST Computer Security Division** (US Department of Commerce). Multi-round open competition, started 2016.

2. **TIMELINE.**
   - **Round 1** (2017–2019): 82 submissions.
   - **Round 2** (2019–2020): 26 candidates.
   - **Round 3** (2020–2022): 7 finalists + 8 alternates.
   - **Round 4** (2022–2024): 4 KEM candidates further studied (NISTIR 8545).
   - **Standardized (Aug 2024):** ML-KEM (FIPS 203), ML-DSA (FIPS 204), SLH-DSA (FIPS 205).
   - **Additional Digital Signatures Round 3** (announced May 14, 2026): 9 candidates — FAEST, HAWK, MAYO, MQOM, QR-UOV, SDitH, SNOVA, SQIsign, UOV. Runs ~2 years (2026–2028). FN-DSA (Falcon) finalized as FIPS 206 in 2025.
   - **Next 5 cycles:** Round 3 Additional Digital Signatures finishes ~2028. Next signature/KEM round if any: 2029. NIST occasionally opens lightweight (closed; ASCON won), threshold (active — see entry 6), and other specialty calls.

3. **URL REF.** [csrc.nist.gov/projects/post-quantum-cryptography](https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/post-quantum-cryptography) / [csrc.nist.gov/projects/pqc-dig-sig](https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/pqc-dig-sig)

4. **URL TO ENTER.** Not open for new submissions to the original PQC track — Round 3 Additional Digital Signatures is the live entry point for a NEW signature scheme.

5. **TERMS OF ENTRY.** Submission package: (i) algorithm specification, (ii) reference implementation, (iii) optimized implementation, (iv) known answer test (KAT) vectors, (v) signed statements (3 of them: submitter, patent owner, implementation owner), (vi) public-availability commitment.

6. **🔴 IP OWNERSHIP — VERBATIM CLAUSE TEXT.** From [NIST PQC IP Statements page](https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/post-quantum-cryptography/post-quantum-cryptography-standardization/submission-requirements/intellectual-property-statements-agreements-di) and [Submission Requirements](https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/post-quantum-cryptography/post-quantum-cryptography-standardization/submission-requirements):

   > "Each submitted algorithm, together with each submitted reference implementation and optimized implementation, must be made freely available for public review and evaluation purposes worldwide during the period of the post-quantum algorithm search and evaluation."
   >
   > "NIST believes it is critical that this process leads to cryptographic standards that can be freely implemented in security technologies and products."
   >
   > "Strong preference for submissions as to which there are commitments to license, without compensation, under reasonable terms and conditions that are demonstrably free of unfair discrimination."
   >
   > Submitter must sign: "(1) statement by the submitter, (2) statement by patent (and patent application) owner(s) (if applicable), and (3) statement by reference/optimized implementations' owner(s)."

   The signed statements include a **commitment to license, under reasonable and demonstrably non-discriminatory terms** — i.e., **royalty-free worldwide license** if your algorithm is selected.

   **Reading:** This is the MOST extreme sharing requirement of any program in this report. Submitting a NEW algorithm to NIST PQC means: (i) the algorithm spec is **public from Day 1** of the submission window, (ii) the reference implementation is public and reviewable, (iii) if selected as standard, you grant the world a royalty-free license to implement, (iv) even if NOT selected, the spec is public and citable forever. **You cannot withdraw** the published spec — once published in NIST's submission archive, it's a permanent reference. Several Round 1 schemes were broken in real-time on the public mailing list (the public reviewing process is brutal but rigorous).

7. **SHARING REQUIREMENTS.** EVERYTHING goes public. The algorithm. The reference impl. The optimized impl. The test vectors. The patent statement. The submitter's name. Public mailing list (`pqc-forum@list.nist.gov`) where the algorithm is dissected.

8. **🔴 DO WE OWN OUR IP — FINAL ANSWER.** **🔴 DANGER for the *algorithm*.** The submitter retains nominal IP but commits to royalty-free worldwide license; in practice the algorithm becomes public-domain-equivalent and a permanent public reference. **🟢 OK for nous as IMPLEMENTER (not submitter)**, however — implementing a NIST-standard algorithm (ML-DSA, ML-KEM, SLH-DSA, FN-DSA, ASCON, future Round 3 winner) inside libsecurity.a is fine and is a powerful marketing line ("nous tracks NIST standards"). **Recommended posture for nous:** Implement, don't submit. The implementation IS yours.

9. **PROOF IN LAYMAN'S TERMS.**
   - Round 1 submitters who became standards: Crystals-Kyber (became ML-KEM, FIPS 203), Crystals-Dilithium (became ML-DSA, FIPS 204), SPHINCS+ (became SLH-DSA, FIPS 205), Falcon (became FN-DSA, FIPS 206).
   - Round 1 submitters whose schemes were broken publicly (some within days): SIKE (broken 2022 by SIDH attack), Rainbow (broken 2022), GeMSS (broken).
   - Round 3 Additional Digital Signatures advancing: FAEST, HAWK, MAYO, MQOM, QR-UOV, SDitH, SNOVA, SQIsign, UOV (announced May 14, 2026).
   - Sources: [NIST PQC Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NIST_Post-Quantum_Cryptography_Standardization), [QuantumInsider Round 3 announcement](https://thequantuminsider.com/2026/05/21/nist-advances-nine-post-quantum-signature-algorithms-to-third-round/).

For a small shop, this is actually a *gift* — your name attached to a NIST reference indefinitely. **Recommended for nous if Joey wants to submit a threshold signature scheme implemented in pure C99.** The cost: open-source reference impl; the value: forever-citation by NIST. Trade is favorable.

9. **PROOF IN LAYMAN'S TERMS.** Not yet, because this is the FIRST formal Threshold Call.

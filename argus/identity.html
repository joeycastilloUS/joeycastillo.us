Two login rails: sovereign keys (overlord + operators, minted by Purcius Genesis) and federated OIDC (users, invited off Slack/Google). One authorization spine: org membership → per-app permission → crew allow-list.

Need

You minted your sovereign root with Purcius Genesis — but it's the substrate key and isn't wired to login yet. Argus today has Slack/Google member login ✓ and a passphrase operator gate, but: no way to log in as the overlord (as you, by your key), no operator tier, no organizations, no per-app permissions, and Settings vanished for member logins. This design unifies all of it: overlord login by key, operators who mint their own keys, orgs as tenants, users invited off Slack, and a redesigned module-specific + global Settings where you set who can touch each app.

Login — two rails, NO passphrase

DECIDED (your comments, Jul 5): the passphrase is retired. You are exactly one of two things: a Purcius operator (you hold a key) or a user (Slack · Discord · Google). Nothing else.

Rail 1 — Sovereign key (overlord + operators): passwordless, key-rooted

The hard part you asked to work out: your Purcius root is cold + Shamir-split (Genesis) — you can't type it into a browser. So the root never touches the console. Instead:

  1. Enrol a device once. The browser/phone generates a non-extractable Ed25519 device key (Web Crypto — private half never leaves the device; same tech as the Registration ceremony). Using your Purcius key (via the Genesis app, or a one-time cold sign) you issue a Warrant for it: sign(root, device_pub ‖ "console"). The Warrant + device pubkey go into your device roster server-side. The root goes back to cold.
  2. Log in every session (no secret typed). Console asks the bridge for a challenge (random nonce, short TTL) → the device key signs the nonce in-browser → the bridge verifies: (a) the signature against the device pubkey, and (b) the device's Warrant chains to your published _key.<fqdn> pubkey, pinned to the mesh's overlord root. Pass → an opaque operator session with full authority.
  3. Strongest variant — the Genesis app as your authenticator. Instead of a browser key, your phone (Genesis app, holding a device key Warranted by your root) signs the challenge via QR/push — a sovereign passkey. Best for a shared/console machine.

This is the "best security" path: Ed25519 challenge–response, device-bound, chained to your sovereign root, verified against the DNS-published + mesh-pinned pubkey. A passkey rooted in your key, not a vendor's. Operators do the identical thing with their own keys, warranted under you.

Rail 2 — Federated OIDC (users): Slack · Discord · Google

Invited users log in with Slack, Discord, or Google — the Argus federation Bind (Slack + Google live ✓; Discord = one more providers.json row). An operator issues a single-use Invite link (the Invite pattern); the user binds their OIDC identity; the bridge mints an opaque session. Login = identity only.

The bug you hit was the passphrase seam — a Slack session unlocked Meraki but the audit/GCP bridges still wanted the passphrase. Retiring the passphrase removes the seam: an operator key session (or an authorized user session) is the single credential every bridge honors.

Authorization — one spine

Every request answers three questions, server-side, in order:

  1. Who? — the identity (key or OIDC session).
  2. Which org? — is this identity a member of an organization that has this app?
  3. May they use this app? — is the identity on the app's per-app access list (extend today's argus_crew_v1 to app_access.{meraki|audit|gcp|…})?

Writes stay separately gated (lab/remediable + capture-before), so "can use the audit app" ≠ "can change your network." The overlord and operators pass all three by virtue of their key.

Settings — redesigned (module-specific + global)

NEW ~design. Today Settings is one operator-only panel keyed to the passphrase. Split it:

Global Settings (overlord)

  • Organizations (create/edit Mendo, Philz, Acme Dev; assign apps + owners).
  • Operators (appoint; view their warrants).
  • The master permission matrix: Users × Apps.
  • Cross-cutting policy (session TTL, invite defaults).

Module Settings (per app — owned by the app)

  • Each app's own config (Meraki: lab keywords, remediable list, op-targets; Audit: check library; GCP: …).
  • Access list for THIS app — the first thing you asked for: pick which users/orgs can enter this module. Reads server-side on every call (the truth, not a browser flag).

First deliverable of this whole design: overlord login by key → Settings returns → per-app access list you can edit. Small, and it unblocks everything you reported.

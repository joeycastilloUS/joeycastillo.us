How we don't have your keys

Plain English. Your browser just generated twelve words. Then it told you to write them down. So how do we not have them? Here is exactly what happened, and what's on our servers.

What just happened on your screen

Your browser generated twelve random words. Not from a server. Not from us. From your own device's random-number generator, using the same hardware randomness your phone uses to scramble your bank app.

Those twelve words exist in three places only:

  • Inside your browser's memory, for a few seconds while you read them
  • On the paper or notebook page where you wrote them down
  • Anywhere else you chose to put them

They were never sent to us. We have no record of them. We do not know what they are.

The locked-safe analogy

If you've ever locked a safe with a combination, you already know how this works.

  1. Your browser made a key. A digital key that locks up your data. This key never leaves your device.
  2. Your browser built a small safe. It put a backup copy of that key inside the safe.
  3. Your browser used your twelve words as the combination to lock the safe. Once locked, the safe is opaque even to the computer that built it.
  4. Your browser sent us the locked safe. We put it in our database.
  5. We have the safe. We never had the combination. The combination existed for a few seconds in your browser, then in your handwriting. Nowhere else.

Where the twelve words came from

Your computer rolled 128 random bits. Like flipping a fair coin 128 times in private. Your CPU has a dedicated hardware random-number generator for exactly this kind of moment.

It converted those bits into twelve words from a published list of 2,048 ordinary English words (a standard called BIP-39). It added a small checksum so you can catch typos when you type them back later.

All of that happened on your device. Nothing left your browser until step 4 in the safe analogy above. And what left in step 4 was only the locked safe, never the combination.

What we actually have on our servers

What we have:

  • The locked safe (a few dozen bytes of useless-looking data)
  • A note saying "this safe belongs to Michelle"
  • Your device's public identifier (which is not your key — it's more like a return address)

What we don't have:

  • The twelve words
  • The key inside the safe
  • Anything that could read your data

A Google engineer with full database access would see locked safes all the way down. So would an attacker who stole our hard drives. So would Joey himself. None of them have the combinations.

What happens when you recover

If your device dies, this is the dance:

  1. You go to a new device. Joey sends you a fresh invitation link.
  2. You type your twelve words into the new device's browser.
  3. The new device sends the twelve words to our server.
  4. For about two seconds, our server uses your twelve words to open the safe. It takes out the key, makes a new safe locked to your new device's identifier, and puts the key inside. Then it deletes your twelve words from memory.
  5. Your new device can now read your data. Your old device's record is marked dead.

That two-second window when we briefly hold your twelve words is the only moment anything plaintext exists on our side. That window only opens when you initiate recovery with your twelve words in hand. We can't open it on our own. We don't have the twelve words.

Why this is math, not policy

"We promise not to look at your data" is a promise. Promises can be broken.

"We mathematically can't look at your data" is different. The math of cryptography says: to read what's inside a locked safe, you need the combination. We don't have the combination. The data on our disks is mathematically indistinguishable from random noise without it.

It isn't that we won't look. It's that there's nothing to look at.

The honest "what ifs"

What if I lose both my device AND the twelve words? Your data is gone. There is no master copy on our end to recover with. This is the cost of not having a vendor to call. Mitigation: write the twelve words down on paper. Put them with the other important papers in your life. Or with a few trusted family members (more on this in the future).
What if I share the twelve words with someone? They have the same access you do. Treat the twelve words like the keys to your house.
What if your servers get hacked? The attacker downloads a pile of locked safes. They can't open them. They got nothing useful.
What if your server is compromised during my recovery? In the ~2 second window when your twelve words are in our server's memory, a sophisticated attacker could in principle observe them. Mitigation: recovery is rare. Brute-force attempts to guess twelve words are rate-limited and locked out. The window is the architecture's only soft spot — and it only opens when you say so.
What if Joey loses his keys? Joey has his own keys for the operator layer of the platform. They are not your keys. He can't break into your stuff even if he wanted to. If he loses his, the platform has a separate recovery path for him that doesn't involve any of your data.
What if Pantheon (the parent organization) is forced by a court order to hand over my data? They would hand over the locked safes. Without the combination — which they do not have — the safes are unreadable. The honest legal answer is: we comply with valid orders by handing over what we have, and what we have is ciphertext.

If you want to dig deeper

  • BIP-39 — the twelve-word standard The wordlist your browser used. Public domain, English, 2048 words.
  • AES-256-GCM — the cipher that locks the safe Authenticated encryption. NSA-approved for TOP SECRET data.
  • PBKDF2 — the math that turns your twelve words into a key Slows down brute-force guessing by 100,000 hash iterations per attempt.
  • X25519 ECDH — how your device unwraps the safe on normal reads The same key-agreement protocol used by Signal, WhatsApp, and modern TLS.

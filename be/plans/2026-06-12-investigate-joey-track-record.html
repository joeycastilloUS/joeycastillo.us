The Through-Line

His father's photographs were seen by millions and nobody read the photographer's name on the back. Joey spent the next forty years writing software seen by millions — running restaurants, certified by McDonald's, breaking onto the news — and nobody read his name either. That was the job: be invisible, be correct, ship the thing that just works. joeycastillo.us is the first time the name goes on the front.

Era 1 — Casa Ricardo's (the apprenticeship)

Born into the work

~1981 onward · era: early

Started working at 5 years old for his mother and father at Casa Ricardo's Photography. His dad's photographs are everywhere — millions of them. At 15, Joey built his father's first computer network: COAX, Microsoft DOS. When Windows 3.11 for Workgroups shipped he upgraded the network, then built an Access database to run the entire company. family businessfirst networkMS-DOSAccess

● first-hand — family history. The tech anchors (COAX Ethernet, MS-DOS, Windows 3.11 for Workgroups, Access) are period-accurate for an early-1990s small-business network.

Era 2 — Custom Business Solutions, Round 1 (the craft)

Field tech to Production Manager in three months

Dec 1995 – ~1997 · era: enterprise

Joined Custom Business Solutions, Inc. (CBS) in December 1995 as a field tech — swapping printers, installing and supporting restaurant POS go-lives. After three months he went in-house as Production Manager: built CBS an Access database, stood up a Lantastic network, and put in a real support system. He wrote the templates and checklists that made POS systems get built and shipped correctly, every time — production discipline, not heroics. CBSproduction managerLantasticprocess

● public-record — CBS is real: Irvine, CA, "since 1994," makers of the NorthStar POS suite (cbsnorthstar.com). ● first-hand — role, timeline, and the production system are Joey's account.

The conviction: own the code

~1997–1998 · era: enterprise

Round 1 CBS resold other people's platforms — PosiTouch and RDC. Joey made the argument that would define the company: we must build our own POS, own all of it, and never be beholden to a vendor. He convinced Art Julian in '97/'98. It took years before they could start the POS — but the decision was made here. strategyown the stackArt Julian

● public-record — PosiTouch / Restaurant Data Concepts (RDC) is real: founded by Bill & Ted Fuller (Gregg's Restaurants, RI), 20,000+ installs, later acquired by Shift4 (2017). A legitimate giant to break away from. ● first-hand — the conviction and the deal with Art Julian.

Era 3 — Koo Koo Roo (plug-and-play)

"Clucky," and a rollout that cost half

~1997–1998, 3–4 months · era: enterprise

At public company Koo Koo Roo, Inc. Joey built software running on the Javelin Wedge ("Clucky") and designed a PosiTouch rollout that was genuinely plug-and-play: a store manager opens the box, plugs it in, taps one button to identify the location, and the unit reconfigures and reboots itself into a working terminal — with printed manuals and color-coded cables. Doing it in-house saved half the cost of the rollout. Rollout complete — and that same week, Norman called. Koo Koo RooJavelin Wedgezero-touch deploycost halved

● public-record — Koo Koo Roo, Inc. was public (NASDAQ KKRO), LA-based flame-broiled-chicken chain, ~3,100 employees / $68M sales in 1997. The Javelin Wedge is a real POS terminal. ● first-hand — "Clucky," the self-configuring rollout, and the 50% saving.

Era 4 — Javelin / Aspeon (the McWedge)

The gate nobody got past — and a year to a McDonald's cert

1998–1999 · era: javelin

Joined Javelin Inc. (public, ticker JVLN) under Norman Campbell, in engineering. Joey was the wall in support: nobody got past him — he made sure the product worked or pinned the issue for engineering. His claim to fame: a full year getting the Javelin Wedge — "The McWedge" — certified by McDonald's Corp. Near the end (~'99) Art Julian came back with a deal. JavelinJVLNNorman CampbellMcDonald's cert

The Wedge terminal line is real. ● first-hand — the McDonald's certification year, "The McWedge," and working under Norman Campbell. (Norman Campbell & Ken Kadlec already live as named Javelin-era voices in the metal kernel.)

Era 5 — CBS, Round 2 (CTO; the whole stack)

50% ownership, and building the POS he'd argued for

~1999 – (long run) · era: enterprise

Art Julian's deal: Joey returns and owns 50% of all software they build together. (He'd been side-lining for Art the whole time at Javelin — writing reports and integrations.) Back at CBS he was CTO the entire time. This is the big chapter: he built the entire restaurant stack — their own POS, many times over (kitchen was integration-only) — the platform that became NorthStar. The conviction from '97/'98 finally shipped as owned code. CTONorthStarown POS50% owner

● public-record — CBS's NorthStar is a real, shipping POS suite (traditional, tabletop, kiosk, web, mobile). ● first-hand — the ownership deal, the CTO tenure, and authorship of the stack.

Norwegian Cruise Line — the first NorthStar build, at sea

NorthStar journey · era: enterprise

The "sea" in land-sea-and-air, literally. For the first build of NorthStar, Joey took his dev team and left the land team behind — went aboard the Norwegian Cruise Line ship to finish it live. They were 20 minutes from being thrown off and replaced with Micros when they finally got the client happy. Non-stop, steely-eyed-missile-man pressure — ship the thing or get put off the boat. NCLfirst NorthStar buildat seabeat Micros

● public-record — CBS publishes the Norwegian Cruise Line Holdings installs NorthStar POS story (Norwegian Escape). Micros (now Oracle Simphony) is a real cruise-POS competitor — the system they displaced. ● first-hand — the 20-minutes-from-Micros finish and taking the dev team aboard.

The Recipe Viewer — 2 weeks to build, still selling today

NorthStar journey · era: enterprise

Rick Smith handed Joey a prototype at the Cheesecake Factory. In two weeks, Joey turned it into the NorthStar Recipe Viewer — and CBS still sells it today. Speed and longevity in one artifact: a two-week build that became a commercial product with a years-long life and an active release train. Recipe ViewerRick Smith2-week buildstill shipping

● public-record (live!) — the NorthStar Recipe Viewer is sold on CBS's site now, actively versioned (v2.1.12), with a published ROI whitepaper; used by Golden Corral (500 locations) and Lazy Dog. A stranger can click and verify the product exists and ships today. ● first-hand — the two-week build and the Rick Smith prototype. This is the strongest single proof on the site: code Joey wrote is still a shipping product years later.

Stacked — the iPad restaurant on the news

2009–2011 · era: enterprise

The headline outcome — but only after many, many NorthStar builds earned the chance. The POS for Stacked: Food Well Built in Torrance / Del Amo: proprietary software integrated from kitchen to an iPad at every table. Joey and Brian Pearson were the two leads — Brian on the Stacked side, Joey on the build. It made the news as a pioneer of tableside iPad ordering.

The pivot. They started building NorthStar for Stacked ~2 months before Steve Jobs announced the iPad — on Android. When the announcement hit (Jan 27, 2010) they were shocked, and pivoted from Android to iPad immediately. That call is why Stacked launched as an iPad pioneer instead of an Android footnote. StackedBrian PearsonAndroid→iPad pivotDel Amo

● public-record — Stacked opened May 31, 2011 at Del Amo, Torrance; covered as an iPad-ordering pioneer; story published on cbsnorthstar.com. The iPad was announced Jan 27, 2010 — the timeline corroborates the pivot. ● first-hand — the co-lead with Brian Pearson and Joey's authorship. Living link: Brian Pearson is now at Mendocino Farms — the same relationship behind the current gated Mendo / Argus work on this very site. A 15-year working bond is itself a trust signal.

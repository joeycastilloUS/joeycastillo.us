Phase 1 Plan — NPS Reports & Food-Borne Illness Handling

Prepared for Mendocino Farms · Guest Relations, Help Desk & IT · builds directly on the approved NPS auto-reply work already in flight. Two deliverables, one plan, both shown as working pictures below.

Summary — where this fits

In our planning meeting we agreed Phase 1 has two jobs: respond to negative guest surveys automatically (with a human approving every guest-facing word), and route after-hours food-illness tickets to the right District Operator, store, and the FSQA team. The auto-reply side is already designed and planned in detail — see 2026-07-03-0000-design-nps-mission1.html and 2026-07-03-0110-plan-nps-mission1-fable5.html (Mission 1: 7 phases, 22 points, roughly $0.007 of model cost per detractor ticket). This plan does not re-plan that work. It plans the two pieces that sit on top of it:

  • Focus A — NPS Reports. The weekly report Guest Relations, Marketing, and the District Operators actually open and read: the score, who's hurting, why, and the guests' own words — per store, per DO, per issue type. Today this is manual; the meeting left "how is the marketing food-illness report generated and delivered?" as an open question. This answers it.
  • Focus B — Food-Borne Illness Handling. The safety-critical path: a guest says they got sick, after hours. The ticket must land with the right DO, the right store, and the FSQA group — and on severe cases, nothing is ever sent automatically. A person approves every action, every time.
Plain-language promise: everything in this document traces back to the 7/2 planning meeting notes and the decisions layered on them since. Nothing here is new scope invented on our side.

Scope

In scope

  • Focus A: a weekly NPS report — score and trend, detractor breakdown per store / per DO / per issue type, and a feed of the actual negative comments. Replaces the manual weekly help-desk report path Brenden owns today, and answers the open "marketing food-illness report" question (illness shows up as a flagged issue type, per store).
  • Focus B: food-illness ticket detection, severity classification, routing to DO + store + FSQA (after-hours aware), and an operator-gated action panel for anything that writes to Zendesk.
  • Verify-first on illness: the meeting noted a foodborne automation may already exist in Zendesk — Brenden is confirming. We map what's live before we build; Argus overlays it, it doesn't duplicate or fight it.
  • Store and DO identity resolved on our side: the survey alert carries a survey id (the "nps-id") in its Store/Order fields with the store name blank — we treat that id as the key and resolve store → DO from Zendesk's own groups (13 "DO – <name>" groups today; the real map moves to Airtable later).

Out of scope (already covered, or deliberately excluded)

  • The NPS auto-reply itself — designed and planned in Mission 1; referenced, not repeated.
  • Fixing the survey alert's field mapping upstream — agreed: we adapt on our side instead.
  • Any write to the "I'm Sorry Code" coupon sheet — Argus only reads the next available code; a person enters it. Always.
  • Any Zendesk write that hasn't been previewed and approved by a person — at any stage, in any mode.
  • Email delivery outside Zendesk — Zendesk owns email for Phase 1.

Approach — how it works, in plain language

  1. Read. Every survey alert and support ticket is read by a deterministic decoder — a small, exact program (not an AI model), so the same ticket always reads the same way. It extracts the facts: score, guest, store id, illness language.
  2. Resolve. The survey id resolves to a store, the store resolves to its District Operator, using the map seeded from Zendesk's own groups.
  3. Judge & draft. Where judgment or wording is needed, a model is used — Google's Gemini today, on your cloud, swappable by design (the model layer is live at api.3-nous.net). Guest-facing wording is trained on Zora's language, so it sounds like Mendo.
  4. Gate. Before anything is written to Zendesk, the operator sees the exact action about to happen — in plain words and as the literal API call — and approves, edits, or rejects it. Then, and only then, it fires. We call this the gated-write pattern; it's the same discipline on every write, and it is non-negotiable on food illness.
  5. Report. The same facts collected along the way roll up into the weekly report — no second data-entry pass, no manual spreadsheet assembly.

Focus A — the Weekly NPS Report

Delivered every Monday morning covering the prior week. One page, three ways to slice it — by store, by District Operator, by issue type — plus the guests' own words, because a number never tells the whole story. Food-illness mentions are flagged in red and cross-linked to their FSQA ticket, which is the "marketing food-illness report" from the meeting, answered inside the weekly report rather than as a separate manual document.

Focus B — Food-Borne Illness Handling

The rule comes first, because it's the whole point: a severe food-illness ticket never triggers an automatic message or an automatic write. Ever. Argus detects, classifies, routes, and prepares — then stops and waits for a person. That's true at every level of automation trust, now and later.

And we build verify-first: the meeting flagged that a foodborne automation may already exist in Zendesk. Brenden is confirming whether it's live and correctly configured. Step one of this focus is mapping what that trigger already does — Argus layers on top of it (severity, DO + store resolution, the gated panel); it does not blindly rebuild or double-fire it.

Constraints — the promises this plan is built around

  • Every Zendesk write is gated. The operator sees the exact call — plain words and the literal API request — approves it, and then it fires. No silent writes, in any mode, at any trust level.
  • Food illness never fires a write without approval. The severe branch is permanently human-approved; it does not graduate to auto as trust builds elsewhere.
  • The coupon sheet is read-only. Argus only peeks the next available "I'm Sorry Code"; a person enters it. Argus never writes that sheet.
  • Tone is Zora's. Guest-facing language is trained on her responses; sign-off authority on wording and automation steps is Elbert / Brenden / Brandi. RJ is not burdened with day-to-day.
  • Zendesk owns email in Phase 1 — alerts come in as tickets, replies go out through Zendesk.
  • Reading is deterministic; judgment is swappable. Tickets are parsed by an exact decoder (same input, same result, every time — not a model). The model layer (Gemini on your Google cloud today) is a swappable socket, live at api.3-nous.net — no vendor lock.
  • We adapt to the survey alert as-is. The survey id arriving in the Store/Order fields is treated as the key; store and DO resolve on our side. No upstream HubSpot fix required.

Unknowns (named, not hidden)

  • Is the existing foodborne trigger live? Brenden is confirming. F0 exists precisely because we don't know yet.
  • Full-score data source. Detractor alerts alone can't compute a true NPS (that needs promoters and passives too). R0 confirms where the full survey counts come from — survey export, report feed, or HubSpot.
  • The current weekly report's exact shape. We match Brenden's manual report before we improve on it; we haven't seen its final format yet.
  • Illness detection recall on history. How reliably illness language is caught across all historical tickets — F1 measures it; a false negative is the one unacceptable miss.
  • Store → DO map coverage. Whether Zendesk's 13 DO groups + historical tickets resolve every live store, until the Airtable map takes over.
  • Delivery preference. Whether Marketing wants the Monday report as a link, an email, or inside a tool they already open.

When To Revisit (the signals that trigger v2)

  • Brenden confirms the foodborne trigger is live and sufficient → F-phases shrink to a thin overlay (severity + DO/store + gate); re-estimate down.
  • A full survey-response feed lands → report v2 with the true score, not an estimate assembled from alerts.
  • Any illness false negative → immediate v2 of the detection branch, before anything else proceeds.
  • The Airtable store → DO map arrives → re-slice the report and routing against the real map; retire the Zendesk seed.
  • Marketing asks for a new slice (daypart, region, item) → report v2; the data assembly is built to add slices cheaply.
  • Operators reject or heavily edit more than ~1 in 3 proposed actions → the judgment layer is wrong, stop and re-plan rather than tune in place.

Approach — how it works, in plain language

One loop drives everything — the same loop from the conversation-as-program design, applied to every ticket, old and new:

  1. Reduce. Every survey alert and support ticket is read by a deterministic decoder — a small, exact program (not an AI model), so the same ticket always reads the same way. It extracts who, what, what's being done, and how it ended. This is real today: it has already read your ten biggest ticket threads and reduced 80,832 characters of raw email back-and-forth to a 20,844-character intent digest — 74% smaller, no meaning lost.
  2. Infer. What does this situation need? First the engine classifies it (illness? missing order? vendor billing?) and retrieves precedent — the past tickets with the same shape and how they got resolved. Only what precedent can't answer goes to an AI model. Pure mechanics first, model last.
  3. Verify. Anything a model proposes is fact-checked against the deterministic read before it's shown to anyone. A suggestion that can't cite its evidence doesn't ship.
  4. Voice. The reply is drafted in your voice — Zora's warmth, Mendo's brand — for a human to approve. (Pulling Zora's actual replies as the tone corpus is an open action item from the 7/2 call; the samples below are honest placeholders until then.)
  5. Replay. After the fact, every handled ticket can be walked step by step — this move, then this response, then resolved — so what worked becomes visible, provable, and reusable. That's where the reports come from: not a spreadsheet pass, a replay of what actually happened.

Gate. Wrapped around all of it: before anything is written to Zendesk or sent to a guest, the operator sees the exact action — plain words and the literal API call — and approves, edits, or rejects. Non-negotiable on food illness.

The Backbone — Every Conversation Is a Program (the backloading engine)

This is the whole plan's engine, not a footnote. A support ticket isn't a record of a conversation — it's a small program that already ran: inputs (the guest, the store, the issue), instructions (each move the guest and your team made), and an exit code (resolved, escalated, lost, recovered). Ten thousand tickets are ten thousand programs that already executed on real infrastructure — your people. Decode them and the archive stops being a pile of text and becomes the most honest account of how Mendo support actually works.

This is already designed — in three reviewed parts:

  • Design v1 — the loop: reduce → infer → verify → voice → replay; mining what worked; the operational codebase; human curation.
  • Design v2 — trees, precedent, drift: real threads branch into multiple issues (tree-shaped programs); inference is classify-and-retrieve before any model runs; drift and new discoveries are where the gold is.
  • Design v3 — the memory: the program library lives in memory, deduped, ranked by what keeps working — precedent lookup in microseconds, and a catalog of what replay buys.

And already proven on your data. Your ten biggest real threads have been decoded end to end ✓ DONE. One concrete example — ticket #341562, a 19-comment, 8,520-character US Foods late-invoice thread for the Cherry Creek store, reduces to a five-step program: request → provide → invoicing → resolved → closed. Requester, vendor contact, account, dollar amount — all extracted, deterministically, for $0 of model cost. Nineteen comments of quoted email, legible in one glance.

The weekly view survives as an automatic rollup — a look back at what was already handled, never the place anyone learns bad news. our recommendation — the 7/2 call asked for the auto-reply and left report generation an open question; real-time-first is the answer we're proposing

Focus B — Food-Borne Illness Handling (multi-channel, gated, severe never auto)

The rule comes first, because it's the whole point: a severe food-illness ticket never triggers an automatic message to a guest and never auto-fires a write. Ever. Argus detects, classifies, routes, notifies your team, and prepares — then stops and waits for a person. That's true at every level of automation trust, now and later. from the 7/2 call

What changed from v1: routing is now multi-channel. Sending email, Slack, and text is the easy part — the discipline is sending the right alert to the right role on the channel that role actually watches at 9:47 PM. FSQA might live in Slack; a District Operator on the road needs a text; the store GM reads email in the morning. The exact channel-per-role matrix is confirmed with Operations before go-live — we do what operations desires, not what's convenient to build. our recommendation — the call specified routing to DO + store; the channel matrix is ours to confirm with ops

Constraints — the promises this plan is built around

  • Every Zendesk write and every guest-facing message is gated. The operator sees the exact reply and the exact call — plain words and the literal API request — approves it, and then it fires. No silent writes, in any mode, at any trust level.
  • Food illness never fires a write without approval. The severe branch is permanently human-approved; it does not graduate to auto as trust builds elsewhere.
  • Internal notifications are automatic; guest words are not. Telling your own team "a detractor just landed" needs no approval — that's the real-time promise. Words to a guest always wait for a human.
  • The channel set is Operations' call. Email, Slack, SMS, Zendesk — per role, confirmed before go-live, changeable any time. We do what ops desires.
  • The coupon sheet is read-only. Argus only peeks the next available "I'm Sorry Code"; a person enters it. Argus never writes that sheet.
  • Tone is Zora's. Guest-facing language is trained on her actual replies (corpus pull = Z0); until then every drafted reply is labeled as sample voice. Sign-off authority on wording and automation steps is Elbert / Brenden / Brandi. RJ is not burdened with day-to-day.
  • Reading is deterministic; judgment is swappable. Tickets are parsed by an exact decoder (same input, same result, every time — not a model), and precedent comes from your own history. The model layer (Gemini on your Google cloud today) is a swappable socket — no vendor lock — and by design it shrinks: the more history the engine holds, the less the model is needed.
  • We adapt to the survey alert as-is. The survey id arriving in the Store/Order fields is treated as the key; store and DO resolve on our side. No upstream HubSpot fix required.

Unknowns (named, not hidden)

  • The ops channel matrix. Who wants Slack, who wants SMS, who wants email, per role and per hour of day — F0 confirms it with Operations before anything goes live.
  • Is the existing foodborne trigger live? Brenden is confirming. F0 exists precisely because we don't know yet.
  • Is "worked" cleanly labelable? The backloading engine mines what worked — but resolved-without-reopen may be a weaker signal than it looks (silent churn, courtesy satisfaction scores). B1's 20-ticket hand-label is the test; if it disagrees with the automated labels, the outcome model gets its own design pass first.
  • Which Zora? Zendesk holds several users named Zora — Z0's first step is confirming which one is the Guest-Relations voice Brenden means, then pulling her public replies as the tone corpus.
  • Full-score data source. Detractor alerts alone can't compute a true NPS (that needs promoters and passives too). R0 confirms where the full survey counts come from.
  • Illness detection recall on history. How reliably illness language is caught across all historical tickets — F1 measures it; a false negative is the one unacceptable miss.
  • Store → DO map coverage. Whether Zendesk's 13 DO groups + historical tickets resolve every live store, until the Airtable map takes over.

When To Revisit (the signals that trigger v3)

  • Operations' channel matrix surprises us (e.g. nobody wants SMS, or stores want a shared channel) → F2 re-shapes around what ops actually said, before build.
  • Brenden confirms the foodborne trigger is live and sufficient → F-phases shrink to a thin overlay (severity + multi-channel + gate); re-estimate down.
  • B1's hand-label disagrees with the automated outcome labels on more than a handful of the 20 → stop; the outcome model needs its own design pass before B2–B4 proceed.
  • A full survey-response feed lands → the live feed and rollup carry the true score, not an estimate assembled from alerts.
  • Any illness false negative → immediate revision of the detection branch, before anything else proceeds.
  • The Zora corpus lands (Z0) → every sample-voice placeholder is retrained and re-reviewed against her actual register; the "sample voice" labels come off only after sign-off.
  • The Airtable store → DO map arrives → re-slice feed and routing against the real map; retire the Zendesk seed.
  • Operators reject or heavily edit more than ~1 in 3 proposed actions → the judgment layer is wrong; stop and re-plan rather than tune in place.

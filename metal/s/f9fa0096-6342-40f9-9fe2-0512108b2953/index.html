The bodies are big — a mean of ~2.2k chars. But most of that mass is transport noise, not signal (next section). This is the single most important thing for the decoder.

The finding that shapes the decoder

82% Email origin → the raw text is mostly email-transport pollution

Ranking the raw vocabulary across all 1000 cases, the top terms are not support content — they're email plumbing and security URL-rewriting:

urldefense ×1414 proofpoint ×1414 https ×3887 godaddy ×2172 digest ×2147 cloud-protect ×2051 mailto ×1353 [URL-defense encoded tokens] ×~9,000

Every link is Proofpoint-URL-Defense-wrapped into long encoded tokens; GoDaddy digests, mailto chains, and quoted-reply history pile on. Roughly half the character mass is machine noise. A naive model over raw bodies would burn tokens reading email headers. This is exactly what understand() is for: atomize past the transport layer to the intent underneath.

The signal underneath

Once the transport noise is set aside, the real operational vocabulary is small and routable:
action ×1835 payload ×1605 release ×1030 block ×718 preview ×702 approve ×655 request ×578 support ×1033 image ×425 paytronix ×353 [agent names] masked [client brands] masked
Read: the recurring shapes are release/approve/preview/block workflows and integration touchpoints (e.g. paytronix, the POS/loyalty side). That's a small, deterministic routing surface — the SF4 "cases as programs" thesis holds against real data.
